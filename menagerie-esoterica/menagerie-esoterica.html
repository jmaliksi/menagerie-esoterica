Menagerie Esoterica - Playtest

What is more fearsome than the monsters of our own making?
Version: 0.10.5

Introduction

The Menagerie Esoterica is a no-prep, narrative-focused tabletop roleplaying game where you and some friends use a deck of tarot cards to craft a monster then play the fools unfortunate enough to face it. It is meant to be played without a GM and without any prep, with the world, the details, and—most importantly—the monster being conjured from whole cloth over the course of the session.

Items Needed

  • a Rider-Waite-Smith style tarot deck
  • a stack of blank index cards
  • pens, markers, crayons, or anything else with which to doodle but not erase
  • a handful of tokens such as coins, beads, or paperclips
  • 1–4 players
  • 90–120 minutes

The Tarot Deck

Instead of dice, Menagerie Esoterica uses a Rider-Waite-Smith style tarot deck for randomization. We are only concerned with the cards as literal playing cards, not necessarily their style or symbolism (though by all means use them if you wish!). If you have a different type of tarot deck, or if you're unsure of what type of deck you have in the first place, so long as there are a total of 78 cards comprised of 22 major arcana and the rest categorized into four suits, and so long as the drawings have a clear up and down, then that deck can be used with this system. A virtual deck can also be used if it allows for randomly drawing cards in reverse.

If you're only familiar with 52 card playing card decks, there is some extra terminology that we use in Menagerie Esoterica:

  • The Minor Arcana are the 56 cards ranked and organized into 4 suits (Wands, Cups, Swords, Pentacles), similar to a 52 card deck
  • The Major Arcana are the 22 additional cards marked by a name and roman numerals, ie II The High Priestess and XIII Death.
  • Cards are illustrated with an orientation that can be viewed right-side up or upside down. If the card is drawn right-side up (from the perspective of the person who drew), it is Upright; if the card is drawn upside down, it is Reversed.

Summary of Play

Menagerie Esoterica can be played in a single session with no prior preparation. It is divided into two phases:

  • In the Creation phase (30–45 minutes), players draw tarot cards to create Truths about the setting and the monster, as well as defining the characters that find themselves in this danger. Players have equal stake in crafting the Situation and will take turns using tarot cards to look up entries in various tables, then using their imaginations to paint in the connective, finer details.
  • In the Engagement phase (60–90 minutes), players take turns performing Gambits, each describing how their character acts in the face of the monster while using Truths to justify the scene, then resolving the action by drawing tarot cards. In addition to deciding success and failure, the tarot cards will pose Queries, asking how the situation progresses or becomes more complicated, thus giving space for players to fill in details and nuance to make their monster truly unique and terrifying. Narrative progression is then recorded symbolically onto the Truth cards.

Truths

At the core of Menagerie Esoterica are Truths. Truths initially get recorded on index cards as a name and icon and serve as springboards and pillars to create more ideas. Over the course of play, players will illustrate more icons on them as the situation evolves, essentially creating their own unique esoterica.

When creating a Truth card, take a blank index card and orient it vertically. Summarize the Truth to a 1-3 word name and write this at the bottom of the card. Take no more than one (1) minute to sketch an icon in the center of the card representing the Truth, leaving plenty of room for more icons to be added. Finally, start a tally with one (1) tally mark at the top of the card.

Setup

Preparing the Deck

Begin by shuffling all Major Arcana and Minor Arcana into one draw pile. When cutting the deck to shuffle, turn one half 180° then proceed as normal. This extra step is to ensure that cards are drawn not just in a random order, but with a random orientation, as well.

Drawing Cards

Upright and Reversed orientations are determined with respect to the drawing player. To avoid ambiguity, it is recommended that the active player place drawn cards face down in front of themselves before revealing them.

After revealing drawn cards (and unless otherwise stated), place them in a discard pile face up next to the draw pile. You may reshuffle the deck at any time; however, it is recommended to only shuffle once the discard pile is clearly taller than the draw pile to minimize interruptions to the flow of play.

Ordering Cards

Some mechanics ask that players order their cards, ie when determining turn order or simply to resolve disagreements. To keep it simple larger numbers beat smaller numbers. For unnumbered cards, Ace is equivalent to 1, Page is 11, Knight is 12, Queen is 13, and King is 14. Draw again to resolve ties.

Optional Rule: Illustrations and Interpretations

At any point when you draw a card and have to refer to a table, ie when creating the situation or monster, you may ignore the table and instead use the illustration and related symbolism for your drawn card as inspiration to answer a prompt.

Creation (30–45 minutes)

Creating the Situation

Before all else, the world must be made.

To set the themes for the session, the players must first answer one question as a group: are you the hunters or the hunted? Is this a monster hunt with a party of competent hunters going into the wilderness to claim their trophy, or is this a cruel happenstance, with unlucky bystanders trying to escape an unexpected horror?

If there is no strong consensus either way, draw one (1) card. If it is Reversed, the party is the hunted. If it is Upright, the party is the hunter.

Tone

Before creating the rest of the Situation's Truths, pause to discuss as a group the tone for the session. Menagerie Esoterica involves passing narrative control to every player at the table so it is important that everyone is invested in telling a similar story. Will this be a horror, with few of you expected to survive the night? Are you embarking on a swashbuckling adventure or a slapstick comedy? Additionally, make sure everyone is on the same page regarding content that should be veiled or avoided entirely.

Truths

Truths paint the setting with specific, evocative details such as the literal physical objects in a scene, peculiarities of the monster's behavior, and even background lore and history. Each Truth is recorded on its own index card, and most importantly, they are mutable. Over the course of the Engagement, Truths will evolve and morph as players answer Queries, bringing in new elements that can help or complicate the situation as it unfolds.

Illustrations

Over the course of play, players will be asked to illustrate new icons on Truth cards as above. Spend no more than 30 seconds sketching these additional icons. While the discussion before committing pen to paper can be spirited and flush with detail, the icons themselves should remain basic and up to interpretation. These discussions should also last no more than a few minutes; the player who is holding the pen and the Truth card (typically whomever drew a tarot card last) breaks any ties and has final say on what gets committed to the page.

Icons are representations, not reminders. A disadvantage one moment can become advantageous the next or vice versa, and a conceptual idea can always suggest tangible objects in a scene or inspire additional lore.

When adding a symbol to a Truth, you may illustrate something entirely new or even modify existing icons by doodling on or around them; however, you may not erase any previously illustrated marks. After adding a new icon to a Truth, add one (1) tally mark to the tally at the top of the card.

Why are you here?

Finally, determine why you are here. Each player draws one (1) card. Go around from highest to lowest, suggesting in one sentence why the group is here. Discuss for a maximum of five (5) minutes, picking and choosing from all suggestions. The goal for this final step is to make sure the players are all in the same headspace before moving on to character creation. Disagreements are best resolved by combining ideas; however, any impasse can also be settled by drawing cards and giving tie-breaking power to the highest draw.

Example

Some reasons why the party may be here:

  • We're exiles on a suicide mission, ready to prove our honor.
  • We're cultists whose ritual has gone horribly right.
  • We're a squad of commandos sent to investigate a ghost ship that suddenly appeared from hyperspace.
  • We're paranormal ghost hunters filming for our show.
  • We're a group of teens on a road trip and our van broke down outside a spooky manor.
  • We're a heist crew doing one last score.
  • We're hunters out to harvest precious resources from the sacred beasts.
  • We're convicts trying to escape a prison ship, innocent or not.

Creating the Monster

Divine Natures of the Beast

The earth rumbles in heart-shaking bass under an echoing snarl that grows from the darkness. What brings the beast here? What is its Drive? The monster is in conflict with the players for a reason, and understanding this reason may help resolve the situation.

Draw one (1) card and place it horizontally in the center of the play area. Use the below table to determine the monster’s Drive according to the drawn card’s suit.

Suit Drive Description
Major Arcana Hunger The creature is on the hunt to fill its belly. The simplest way to turn it away is to sate this hunger.
Cups Territory The creature is defending its chosen territory, whether that territory was chosen a millennia ago or just this morning.
Pentacles Treasure The creature desires valuables to bring back to its nest.
Swords Bond The creature has become aggressive in order to impress, defend, or perhaps avenge another creature.
Wands Chaos The creature's drive is ultimately a mystery that is only looking for a solution, not understanding.

Engagement (60–90 minutes)

Summary

Todo

card illustrations

Example

The Engagement begins with players setting a Goal and drawing one (1) card face down to represent Fate and to hold their progress tokens.

After the Goal is set, play is organized into rounds, called Antes. At the start of each Ante, a Query is posed, introducing context and complications into the Engagement. Players then take turns each performing one (1) Gambit, where they describe what their character does in the face of the monster, and how they take advantage of Truths to further progress to the Goal.

A Gambit's outcome is determined by drawing tarot cards. Positive outcomes further progress to the Goal, while negative outcomes introduce complications. Regardless of outcome, the triumphs and tribulations are illustrated as a new icon on relevant Truth cards, modifying their meaning.

The Engagement comes to a close at the end of Ante 4, whereafter players Take Decisive Action. Their Fate is revealed, prompting one final Query to end the game.

Set the Goal

Start the Engagement by deciding on a Goal. We know how the party got here, but with a monster suddenly threatening them, we need to know what the party intends to do now.

Example

Examples of Goals might be:

  • The party is here to hunt the monster and put an end to its reign of terror, and so hunt it they shall.
  • Scientists want to study the monster and create a theme park so the party is here to capture it live.
  • A loved one was transformed into the monster, so the party wants to mollify and turn it.
  • The monster has killed everyone else, and the party only wants to survive long enough to take the last escape pod.
  • The monster guards the Holy Grail. All the party has to do is take it and make sure the monster does not follow.
  • A villain is making his escape and summons a monster to buy time. The party must contend with the threat while capturing their target.
  • The monster kidnapped the princess. The party must rescue her and return her to the king unscathed.

The Goal may not necessarily be about killing the monster, and so we do not define the Engagement in terms of hit points; instead we define the Engagement in terms of progress and Fate. Draw one (1) card but keep it face down. Place it to the right of the monster's core. This is the Fate deck. As the Ante increases, more face-down cards will be added to this pile, only to be revealed at the end of the Engagement when the party Takes Decisive Action.

Players may change the Goal at any time, so long as everyone is in agreement—after all, the difference between flight and fight rides a fine knife's edge.

The Ante

The Ante represents the rising stakes as the players get closer to their Goal but draw the increasing attention and ire of the monster. The Ante starts at 1 at the beginning of the Engagement, and will increase over the course of the game to a maximum of four (4). The Ante is tracked by the number of cards in the Fate deck.

When all tokens have been moved from the Query card to the Fate deck, the current round ends and the Ante is increased. Remove the tokens from the Fate deck and draw one (1) tarot card. Keeping it face-down, place it atop the Fate deck. Your Fate is slowly built over the course of the Engagement, but it is only fully manifested and shown at the end.

At the end of every Ante, find the Truth (including Character Truths) with the most tally marks and flip it face down. This Truth can no longer be referenced until it is turned face up again. Envision the complication that rendered your most used touchpoint null. If any other Truths were face down, they may now be turned face up. Envision the circumstance that brings these missing Truths back into play.

At the end of Ante 4 (ie when there are four (4) cards in the Fate deck and no progress tokens remain on the Query card), the party must Take Decisive Action, bringing the Engagement to a close by revealing the cards in the Fate deck.

The Query

Each Ante begins with a Query which poses a question that changes the current situation and must be solved or worked around by the party. At the start of the round when a new Query must be posed, draw one (1) card. This is the Query card. Use it in conjunction with the Ante to determine the Query.

Player Turn

Once the Ante's initial Query has been answered, players take turns performing Gambits to make progress on the party's Goal in the face of the monster. Turn order is not set; however, there are two rules players must follow to ensure space for everyone to take center stage and shine:

  1. A player may not perform two Gambits in a row.
  2. Any player who did not perform a Gambit in one Ante must be first to act in the next Ante.

The player turn can be broken into three steps:

  1. Perform a Gambit. The player describes the action their character does, referencing one or more Truths to explain why their action may be effective.
  2. Read the Outcome. The player draws 1–3 tarot cards, with the orientation of drawn cards determining success or failure.
  3. Illustrate Icons. The player answers Queries posed by the drawn cards by illustrating new icons on relevant Truth cards.

Gambits

Gambits are the risky, audacious actions that a character does in the face of the monster, the outcomes of which are resolved by drawing tarot cards. However, the monster is a force of nature and without a deliberate approach, no progress will be made; therefore, a Gambit is composed not only of the action, but must also be accompanied by one or more Truths.

Actions taken when not under threat of the monster such as walking, talking, and observing are considered free in that we don't need to draw to resolve them. The player characters are at a baseline of competence; if the monster can't directly or indirectly thwart the player's action then there is no reason for them to fail.

To perform a Gambit, the player gathers at least one (1) and at most three (3) Truth cards. The player then describes how their character acts, using the gathered Truths as touch points to explain what threat or complication is being addressed, why their character is able to perform the action, and/or why they think it should be effective. Players are invited to conjure new details, lore, backstory, etc. so long as they make sense within the gathered Truths' context and so long as previously established details are not contradicted.

Examples of Gambits might include:

  • Leonid tries to hide from the monster. He gathers the Mind Sense Truth, saying he's hiding by imagining himself far away by the ocean. He also gathers the Ancient High School Truth, saying that he's actually hiding in the bathroom and flushing a toilet to help him visualize waves.
  • Kim shoots an arrow at the monster's eyes.   • Pilbin tries to evade the monster and capture its true master by running along the rooftops. They gather the Tentacles Truth, their own Specialty of Parkour, and the Opaque Fog Truth, saying that the monster's slimy limbs will desiccate if they rise out of the mists.
  • Veronicole closes her eyes and bows. She gathers the Invisibility Truth and the Respected Old Ways Truth, saying that the old folk tales emphasize respect and mention invisible creatures simply wanting to hide, and that by closing her eyes of her own volition, she shows respect for the monster's desire to be unseen.
  • Rydell tries to slash the monster. They gather their own Truth of Swordsman to justify proficiency with a blade, as well as the Monster Hunting Tradition to say that it's well known that iron blades can cut the otherworldly.
  • Hawthorne looks for a secret passage out of the trap room. If no cards were drawn upright, then the Gambit was a disaster. Whether by poor assumptions, bad footing, or simple bad luck, the situation worsens. Envision the catastrophe, using the suit of any drawn card to determine its nature.

Suit Query
Major Arcana 0-X How has the goal slipped further away?
Major Arcana XI–XXI What did we misunderstand about the monster?
Swords What trick did the monster pull?
Wands How were you cornered?
Cups Why did you falter?
Pentacles Who has been harmed?

On at least one (1) gathered Truth, increase the tally by one (1) and illustrate a new icon representing the consequences of disaster.

Next, find the Truth with the fewest tally marks regardless of if it was gathered for the Gambit. Choose only one (1) in case of ties. Increase the tally by one (1) and illustrate a new icon representing how it unexpectedly compounded your misfortune.

Finally, find the Truth with the most tally marks and turn it face down. Choose only one (1) in ase of ties. This Truth may no longer be referenced in Gambits until the next Ante. Envision how the disaster renders another of party's most used touchpoint null.

Bonds

After revealing the outcome of their Gambit but before illustrating Truths, the active player may Forge a Bond to draw one (1) additional card, even if the maximum three (3) cards were drawn. Choose at least one (1) other willing player and a Bond from the table below. Answer the Query as a pair, envisioning it in the context of a flashback when the original Bond was cemented or challenged. Increase the tally by one (1) and illustrate a new icon on both Character Truths to represent this memory.

After finishing the illustration, draw and reveal one (1) card, and use it in addition to the previously revealed cards to determine the Gambit’s new outcome.

Bond Query
Love Who has always been with you through thick and thin?
Mentor Who prepared you for this moment?
Duty Who must come to your aid, by oath or by blood?
Rival Who has always pushed you to be one step ahead?

Each Bond may only be Forged once (1) per Engagement. An existing Bond may be invoked for all future Gambits; however, it can no longer be used to exceed the maximum three (3) card draw.

If playing solo, you may envision a non-player character in flashback that has fulfilled this bond instead.

Take Decisive Action

At the end of Ante 4, the party must Take Decisive Action and collectively describe how they bring the encounter to a close. Each player takes turns revealing each card in the Fate Deck. Once all cards are revealed, use the table to determine the Engagement's Outcome and the Final Query.

Card Outcome Query
At least two (2) Upright Major Arcana Triumph What was celebrated?
At least one (1) Upright Major Arcana Hard Won Victory What scars remain?
At least one (1) Upright Minor Arcana Pyrrhic Victory What was sacrificed?
All cards Reversed Tragedy and Misfortune What was lost?

Based on the Outcome, envision how the Decisive Action successfully accomplishes the goal; or how the Decisive Action takes a turn for the unfortunate at the finish line. Regardless, the Engagement comes to a close.

Take a moment to process what happened and to look at the Truth cards around you. When you are all ready, answer the Final Query as a group and envision the epilogue of this monstrous encounter. Now you may Rest.
